Essay

Digitisation as Cultural Power: Open GLAM, AI, and the Future of Cultural Memory

Course: Digital Humanities · Word count: 1424
Author: Shiqi Ge

Digitisation, selection, and cultural power

Discussions around GLAM digitisation initially appeared to me as largely technical matters with scanning fragile artefacts, producing stable file formats, and sustaining workflows. The early course materials seemed to confirm this view, with their emphasis on standards, primary digitisation, and infrastructures such as Europeana. But as the module unfolded, and especially as the examples from Tallinn, Rijeka, the Thessaloniki pilot, and the Tate and Pompidou came into view, that neat picture began to unravel. I started to see that digitisation is not simply a neutral upgrade. It is a cultural and political act that shapes what becomes visible, what survives, and what quietly slips out of circulation. It does not merely expand access; it also sets the terms on which culture will exist in the future.

A key realisation for me was that digitisation is unavoidably selective, and therefore a form of power. To digitise an artefact is, in a sense, to grant it an afterlife and make it searchable, linkable, and eligible for circulation across platforms and aggregators. Europeana demonstrates how this selective logic now operates on a continental scale by connecting tens of millions of objects across thousands of institutions. Material that remains undigitised, by contrast, faces disappearance in both physical and digital cultural domains. Expanded digital access can mask profound unevenness, leaving minority cultures and local archives underrepresented despite the rhetoric of openness (King, Stark & Cooke, 2016). Limited institutional resources further amplify this imbalance, favouring dominant languages and heavily documented collections. Digitisation is therefore not passive preservation. Instead, it actively reshapes cultural hierarchies by determining what is discoverable, searchable, and re-usable.

Platforms, AI, and algorithmic gatekeeping

The discussions in Tallinn made this asymmetry feel even sharper. In a cultural landscape now mediated by search engines, social media feeds, aggregators and AI systems, platforms have become de facto gatekeepers. Algorithms decide what is recommended or included in training datasets, often reproducing existing structural inequalities rather than alleviating them (Noble, 2014). Material that is undigitised, or poorly described, effectively does not exist in algorithmic terms. Both Tallinn and the Council of Europe’s seminar in Rijeka warned that without equitable digitisation and interoperable infrastructures, digital culture risks reinforcing these hierarchies. The Council of Europe’s Recommendation on Big Data for Culture, Literacy and Democracy (2017) underscores this danger by articulating a right to remain “inscrutable”, a right to resist continuous profiling and prediction. It reframes cultural participation not as a personalised optimisation challenge but as a democratic right.

Open GLAM and cultural infrastructures

Seen in this light, the urgency of Open GLAM becomes clearer. Open standards, open metadata and open-access infrastructures are not simply technical preferences, instead they function as counterweights to platform-driven inequalities. Digitisation must be accompanied by policies that safeguard diversity and citizen agency in an AI-mediated environment, as noted by the Council of Europe (n.d.). The Tate and Pompidou case studies show how open institutional infrastructures can broaden public participation and reduce reliance on opaque commercial systems (Alshawaaf & Lee, 2020). These examples suggest that open frameworks are not auxiliary enhancements. They are the foundations on which cultural visibility and public cultural participation depend.

The Council of Europe’s insistence on inscrutability also unsettles the assumption that cultural experiences must be endlessly personalised. Cultural meaning thrives on unpredictability: on serendipity, discomfort, and discovery. These are the qualities predictive analytics struggle to reproduce. Fully personalised systems risk constructing cultural echo chambers and narrowing the aesthetic field. A world filtered entirely through algorithmic similarity would be culturally homogeneous and intellectually stagnant.

Policy, unpredictability, and cultural democracy

Seen together, these debates shift the policy implications. If digital platforms increasingly mediate access to culture, GLAM institutions must consider how to safeguard unpredictability and plurality within algorithmic ecosystems. Should recommendation systems be designed to include moments of “surprise”? Should digitisation priorities foreground endangered or underrepresented cultures to counterbalance algorithmic bias? Should funding shift toward infrastructures that guarantee digital discoverability rather than prioritising traditional exhibition practices? These are not technical adjustments, but political decisions about cultural democracy. They are also about whether digitisation expands cultural agency or, perhaps just as easily, restricts it, and whether Open GLAM can realistically counterbalance platform power.

Artistic practice and infrastructural critique

The course also changed how I think about art in an AI-saturated environment. I began to see how artistic practice can function as a critical method for making technological infrastructures visible. Anatomy of an AI System, created by Crawford and Joler, was particularly striking to me: it maps the labour, extraction and data flows underlying the Amazon Echo (Crawford & Joler, 2018). Its presentation at the Council of Europe’s expert seminar in Rijeka shows how artistic interpretation is now recognised in policy spaces as a legitimate way of exposing the political and material realities of AI systems (Council of Europe, 2018). A similar sensibility appeared in the Thessaloniki pilot: the 3D reconstructions weren’t aimed at producing seamless virtual pasts but at foregrounding the modelling assumptions, uncertainties and interpretative decisions behind them (Valeonti, 2022). Rather than offering polished replicas, the project highlighted the epistemic labour behind them, showing how artistic and curatorial choices shape what can be culturally imagined in digital form.

Frameworks for interpretive transparency

Both the London Charter and the Seville Principles reinforce this orientation by requiring digital heritage visualisations to make their interpretive foundations visible. The London Charter emphasises “intellectual transparency” where users should be able to distinguish evidence from hypothesis and understand varying degrees of probability, supported by documentation of research sources and interpretative decisions (London Charter, 2009). The Seville Principles likewise stress authenticity and scientific transparency, requiring virtual reconstructions to differentiate clearly between in situ material, physical reconstruction and purely virtual restoration (López-Menchero & Grande, 2013). Together, these frameworks insist that digitisation involves choices: what to emphasise or leave out, which narratives to foreground, and even how much uncertainty to expose. They also challenge the myth of technological neutrality by asking digital models to expose, rather than hide, their interpretive layers.

Commercial platforms and cultural activation events

Still, even the best frameworks cannot ensure impact if public cultural habits do not shift. This becomes most visible when considering commercial platforms. Commercialisation is often framed as a threat to cultural integrity, but the course encouraged me to see it instead as a condition shaped by everyday digital behaviours. Governments may build policy infrastructures, and GLAM institutions may create interoperable collections, but private platforms supply the technological capacity and distribution pathways through which most people now encounter culture. A sustainable digital cultural ecosystem ultimately depends not only on institutions but on how publics choose to engage with the heritage available to them.

This realisation led me to what I call cultural activation events—moments when cultural materials abruptly gain visibility through digital circulation. These events do not replace long-term institutional planning, but they can spark attention and generate momentum. They include, for instance, a museum TikTok trend introducing an artefact to younger audiences, a digitised manuscript that becomes a meme, or an online exhibition that unexpectedly goes viral. Such phenomena reveal that cultural participation today is not linear or exclusively institutional; it is hybrid, algorithmic, and socially distributed across many digital spaces.

Conclusion: digitisation as cultural responsibility

From this perspective, digitisation becomes not only a preservation tool but also a way of imagining cultural futures. It determines what survives, how it circulates, and who can take part in shaping cultural meaning. AI compels us to defend unpredictability, diversity and multiplicity within cultural systems. Platformisation forces us to rethink how access is organised beyond institutional walls. Commercial dynamics, meanwhile, raise new questions about what meaningful participation even looks like. GLAM institutions, situated at the intersection of policy, technology and public behaviour, must navigate these pressures while upholding democratic responsibilities.

My own work during the course such as creating digital artefacts, building websites and taking part in collaborative discussions really reinforced this perspective for me. Digital practice, I realised, is not merely technical; it is as well interpretative and ethical. Every design decision, from interface layout to metadata structure, subtly shapes how audiences encounter and remember cultural materials. Even small interventions become part of a broader process of deciding which cultural forms will remain visible, valued or forgotten.

If digitisation is the mechanism through which culture enters the future, then the future is already being shaped, through curatorial choices, platform behaviours, institutional policies and everyday user interactions. Thus for me, this means that digitisation is no longer a neutral procedure but a cultural responsibility. As digital humanists, our task is not simply to build systems but to cultivate environments that honour pluralism, transparency and public agency. Culture does not preserve itself; it is preserved, transformed and reimagined through the collective decisions we make now.
